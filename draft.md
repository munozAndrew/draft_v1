Hypothesis
Prediction markets work by letting people trade on the outcome of a question, and the final price is basically the crowd’s probability estimate. The general idea is that if enough people trade, the price ends up being a pretty good prediction.What I’m testing is whether the wording of the question matters. Specifically: do markets with longer, more complex questions get priced less accurately than short, simple ones? A short question like “Will CPI rise more than 0.1% in August?” is pretty straightforward. A longer one like “Will the yield of 10-year U.S. treasury notes be between 3.55 and 3.57 on May 1, 2023?” has more moving parts: a specific instrument, a narrow numeric band, and a specific date. My hypothesis is that questions like that are harder to price accurately because there are more ways to misunderstand what’s being asked.
Data
I’m using Kalshi only. I considered Polymarket, but I excluded it because it doesn’t have a clean, structured category system (for the first draft). Kalshi has an event_ticker field with consistent prefixes (like FED, KXCPIYOY, HIGHNY) that make it easy to group markets. Polymarket mostly gives you free-text questions and a URL slug, so category filtering becomes keyword matching, which is noisier and harder to defend. To build categories, I ran extract_categories.py, which scans all Kalshi parquet files and extracts every unique event_ticker prefix (the substring before the first dash, like FED from FED-2024-MAR-25). This produced a CSV with 5,562 unique prefixes, their market counts, and sample titles. Then I wrote build_domains.py to map these prefixes into four domains using simple prefix rules (for example, anything starting with HIGH goes to Weather, and things like CPI or FED go to Economics / Finance). Prefixes that did not match a domain (crypto, one offs.) were dropped. The output is domain_assignments.csv, which draft_one.py reads as a lookup table.
The four focus domains and rough usable market counts are:
Economics / Finance: ~64,000
Weather: ~30,000
Politics / Government: ~12,000
Entertainment / Awards: ~7,000
Sports and parlay markets were excluded because their titles are basically auto generated templates, not real “questions” in the same sense.
Metrics
For accuracy, I use the Brier Score:
Brier = (predicted_probability − actual_outcome)^2
Here, predicted_probability is last_price / 100 where last_price is the last traded price in cents (0–99). The actual_outcome is 1 if result = yes and 0 if result = no. Lower Brier means more accurate. For example, pricing something at 95% and being right gives (0.95 − 1)^2 = 0.0025, while a 50/50 guess gives 0.25. For complexity, I use word count. I count whitespace separated words in the market title.
Results
The main result is that longer questions are priced less accurately. In Figure 1, I transformed the focus markets into word count quintiles and plotted the mean Brier Score per bucket. The pattern is almost perfectly monotonic. The shortest questions (about 5–12 words) have mean Brier ≈ 0.021, while the longest (about 17–49 words) have mean Brier ≈ 0.065, which is more than 3 times worse. The Spearman rank correlation between word count and Brier Score is ρ = 0.319, so complexity isn’t the only thing affecting the Brier Score.
Figure 2. shows the domain breakdown, and it lines up well with the hypothesis. Economics / Finance has the longest average questions (about 14.9 words) and the worst accuracy (Brier ≈ 0.043). Entertainment / Awards is next (about 12.8 words, Brier ≈ 0.039). Weather has shorter questions (about 12.0 words) and better accuracy (Brier ≈ 0.017). Politics / Government is the interesting one: medium-ish length (about 13.2 words) but the best accuracy (Brier ≈ 0.013). My best explanation is that a lot of political questions track slow moving, heavily reported numbers (like approval ratings), so traders have frequent updates  even if the title is a bit detailed.
Figure 3. Each event_ticker prefix is a dot, with mean word count on the x-axis and mean Brier on the y-axis. The OLS trend line slopes upward, meaning categories with longer average titles tend to be less accurate overall.
Discussion
Overall, the hypothesis is supported. In the four domains I focused on, longer question titles are consistently associated with worse prediction accuracy. You can see it at the market level (ρ = 0.319), at the domain level (Figure 2), and at the category level (Figure 3).Two things probably explain this. First is misreading. The more specific a question is, like narrow price bands, exact timestamps, or specific instruments, the more ways there are to get one detail wrong and misprice it. Second is participation. If a question feels complicated, fewer traders may feel confident enough to trade on it, so pricing errors do not get corrected as quickly.Politics being the exception still fits the overall story. It suggests complexity matters more when the underlying thing is already hard to predict or moves quickly. If the number being tracked is public, changes slowly, and is updated all the time, traders can still price it accurately even if the question is a bit longer.
Limitations
One limitation is last_price timing. The final trade could happen after the outcome is basically known but before Kalshi officially resolves the market. That would make some markets look more accurate than they really were in advance, especially small threshold markets where prices jump as soon as a number gets published. Also, word count is a rough complexity. It captures length, but not how many conditions are packed in, how precise the numbers are, or whether the question is confusing even when short.
